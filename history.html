A Rich History of Space Science

1800s 1920s 1940s 1950s 1960s 1970s 1980s 1990s 2000s 2010s

1800's

1883 JHU Physics professor Henry A. Rowland publishes his invention of a concave grating, which would become a basic tool of observations of space. Rowland came to JHU in 1876 as the Chair of the physics department and was awarded an honorary doctorate in 1880. He became the president of the American Physical Society.

1884 Simon Newcomb is professor of mathematics and astronomy at Johns Hopkins University. Created the world standard of astronomical constants and orbit parameters.

1920's

1929 Joseph Ames named President of Johns Hopkins University and serves until 1935. A Professor of Physics, he was a founding member of the National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics (NACA, which later became NASA) and he served on it for 24 years. The NASA Ames Research Center and Ames Hall on the Homewood campus are named for him. Ames earned a B.A. and doctorate degree from Johns Hopkins.

1940's

1942 Three months after the US entered World War II, APL was created to mobilize academic resources to address wartime challenges.

1946 APL begins high-altitude research using rockets.

1948 The first photo of Earth from space is made by an APL camera on a V2 rocket (appeared in National Geographic magazine in October 1950).

1948-1952 Ralph Alpher and Robert Herman at APL develop the Big Bang model of cosmology and prediction the existence of the Cosmic Microwave Background radiation.

1950's

1955 APL leads a consortium of academic and industrial organizations to produce standard rockets.

1957 APL tracks Sputnik to produce its orbital parameters.

1958 APL developes the world’s first Doppler-based, all-weather, satellite navigation system

1960's

1960-1972 A small group of professors, led by research professor William G. Fastie, form a fledgling astrophysics effort within the Physics department. This effort concentrated on ultraviolet aeronomy and astronomy from space, initially from sub-orbital sounding rocket experiments, but also resulting in the presence of an ultraviolet spectrometer that was carried aboard the Apollo 17 mission in December 1972 to search for a residual lunar atmosphere.

1963 APL demonstrates first gravity-gradient stabilized spacecraft orbit.

1964 APL develops the NASA Beacon Explorer satellite.

1965 The first of three APL-built GEOS geodetic research satellites is launched

1970's

1975 APL's GEOS C spacecraft demonstrates first satellite-to-satellite tracking

1977 APL-built Low Energy Charged Particle instrument launches aboard Voyager to explore the outer planets

1978 The International Ultraviolet Explorer satellite was also launched this year, and JHU professors Warren Moos, Paul Feldman, and Dick Henry were active users of this NASA facility, building on their JHU rocket program experience.

1979 Professor Arthur F. Davidsen received the Helen B. Warner prize of the American Astronomical Society for a 1977 JHU sounding rocket flight that obtained the first ultraviolet spectrum of a quasi-stellar object.

1980's

1980 The Hopkins Ultraviolet Telescope (HUT) project was accepted for development by NASA. The concept, for a 0.9 m telescope to fly multiple times on the space shuttle as an attached payload, was a direct descendant of the rocket program at JHU. Professor Arthur Davidsen was the principal investigator and Paul Feldman and Dick Henry were co-investigators. Although the telescope did not ultimately fly until 1990, this selection catapulted JHU into a leadership position in space astrophysics. (Note for context: the first space shuttle flight did not occur until April 1981.)

1981 A Johns Hopkins/Associated Universities for Research in Astronomy proposal was selected by NASA to host the Space Telescope Science Institute (STScI) on the Homewood campus of JHU. Arthur Davidsen and JHU president Steven Muller were key players in the effort. Although the STScI is an independent entity, its location on the JHU campus forever changed the landscape of astrophysics at JHU.

1983 The Steven Muller building, which houses the STScI, was dedicated on June 15th.

1984 APL's Charge Composition Explorer spacecraft launches as part of the Active Magnetic Particle Tracer Explorer (AMPTE) mission, a three-nation, three-spacecraft effort to study plasma phenomena in near-Earth space

1984 On April 26, JHU approved the renaming of the Physics Department to be the Department of Physics and Astronomy, recognizing the growing importance of astrophysics as a separate emphasis of the department.

1985 APL's GEOSAT radar altimeter advances space geodesy

1988 In February, the Far Ultraviolet Spectroscopic Explorer (FUSE) mission was selected by NASA for a development study. The principal investigator was professor Warren Moos

1988 JHU was the lead local host institution for the triennial meeting of the International Astronomical Union, held in August at the Baltimore convention center.

1989 A JHU-led proposal is accepted to host the Maryland Space Grant Consortium in the department. Professor Richard Conn Henry was and is the director. This is part of the National Space Grant program established by congress in 1988, and supports state and national goals in education and creating a diverse and technically literate work force.

1989 APL operates the integrated sensor module in Delta 183, paving the way for the Midcourse Space Experiment (MSX)

1989 The Galileo spacecraft carries an APL particle detector on a mission to Jupiter

1990's

1990 STS-31 launched on Apr. 24th, with shuttle Discovery carrying the Hubble Space Telescope to orbit. JHU professors Holland Ford and Arthur Davidsen were co-investigators of one of the original Hubble science instruments, the Faint Object Spectrograph (FOS)

1990 In June, the department left its Rowland Hall home and moved into the Bloomberg Center for Physics and Astronomy, across San Martin Drive from the STScI. The $35M building more than doubled the space available for labs and offices and included a high bay and clean room facility for handling space payloads. To keep the “Rowland” name associated with the department, the name was officially changed to the Henry A. Rowland Department of Physics and Astronomy.

1990 On December 2, space shuttle Columbia rocketed into orbit carrying two important JHU contributions on STS-35, the Astro-1 mission. The first was the Hopkins Ultraviolet Telescope, and the second was Samuel T. Durrance, a payload specialist and JHU research scientist and HUT team member.

1993 The Morris W. Offit telescope, named for a former chairman of the JHU board of trustees, takes its place atop of the Bloomberg building, as part of the Maryland Space Grant Observatory.

1993 On December 2, space shuttle Endeavour launched on the first Hubble Servicing Mission (STS-61). JHU professor Holland Ford was the project scientist of COSTAR, the corrective optics package that restored Hubble’s image clarity.

1994 Holland Ford and colleagues use the repaired Hubble and the FOS instrument to confirm the presence of a supermassive black hole in the center of elliptical galaxy M87.

1994 The FUSE mission was restructured, and the new FUSE mission was accepted and fast-tracked by NASA.

1995 After Astro-1, the HUT telescope and spectrograph were refurbished at JHU and on March 2, space shuttle Endeavour launched on STS-67, the Astro-2 mission, again carrying HUT and payload specialist Samuel Durrance. The 16-day mission was nearly flawless, quite in contrast to Astro-1, and resulted in a treasure trove of data, including an exceptional observation that resulted in a unique measurement of the characteristics of the intergalactic medium, a long-sought prize of PI Arthur Davidsen. (Interestingly, this was also John Grunsfeld’s first flight. John went on the fly several of the subsequent Hubble servicing missions, including SM4; see 2009.)

1997 STS-82 (shuttle Discovery) launched on Feb. 11th on the second Hubble servicing mission. This mission carried the Space Telescope Imaging Spectrograph to Hubble, and professor Warren Moos was a co-investigator.

1997 APL’s Advanced Composition Explorer (ACE) spacecraft is launched

1998 May saw the official “first light” of the Sloan Digital Sky Survey (SDSS) telescope at the Apache Point Observatory in New Mexico. SDSS has gone on to become a central focus of research throughout the astronomical community over the last decade. JHU’s key contributions included a multi-fiber spectrograph, key elements of the database and software to archive and mine the data, and the calibration telescope (which began life in the observatory on top of Bloomberg).

1999 June 24th saw the launch of the Far Ultraviolet Spectroscopic Explorer (FUSE) mission on a Delta-II rocket from Cape Canaveral. Warren Moos was the PI, and the mission operations were directed from the Bloomberg Center control room on the Homewood campus. For much of this time, research professor William Blair was the Chief of FUSE Operations. FUSE operated for 8 years, with a team of anywhere from 45 to 25 JHU scientists and engineers controlling all aspects of the mission.

1999 APL is awarded the NASA Mercury Surface, Space Environment, Geochemistry, and Ranging (MESSENGER) mission to Mercury

2000's

2000 October 5th marked the dedication and official start of the Sloan Digital Sky Survey, part 1 (SDSS-I, 2000 – 2005)

2001 In March, the Hopkins Ultraviolet Telescope was moved to the National Air & Space museum in Washington DC, as part of a permanent exhibit called, “Exploring the Universe.”

2002 JHU Professor Riccardo Giacconi of Physics and Astronomy is awarded the Nobel Prize for, “for pioneering contributions to astrophysics, which have led to the discovery of cosmic X-ray sources.”

2002 STS-109 (Columbia) launched on Hubble Servicing Mission 3B, carrying the Advanced Camera for Surveys (ACS) to Hubble. Principal investigator professor Holland Ford led the team that developed the ACS, which increased Hubble’s “discovery space” by a factor of 10. ACS experienced an electrical failure on Jan. 27, 2007, but was ultimately repaired on-orbit during STS-125 (May 2009). At this writing, ACS continues to be a key instrument in Hubble’s arsenal of scientific instruments.

2004 APL launches MESSENGER, the first Mercury-orbiting spacecraft

2005 July marked the official start of the Sloan Digital Sky Survey, part 2 (SDSS-II, 2005 – 2008). Professor Alex Szalay and CAS manager James Crocker played key roles in SDSS, one of the most successful sky surveys performed to date.

2005 Charles L. Bennett joins the Johns Hopkins faculty, bringing with him the leadership of the Wilkinson Microwave Anisotropy (WMAP) space mission.

2005 Adam Riess joins the JHU Department of Physics and Astronomy and goes on to win the Nobel Prize in 2011.

2006 APL Solar Terrestrial Relations Observatory (STEREO) spacecraft launches on a mission to image the Sun in 3-D

2006 APL's Compact Reconnaissance Imaging Spectrometer for Mars (CRISM) instrument looks for water on Mars

2006 Adam Riess, JHU Professor of Physics and Astronomy, is awarded the Shaw Prize in Astronomy for providing evidence that the expansion of the universe is accelerating.

2006 Charles L. Bennett, JHU Professor of Physics and Astronomy, is awarded a portion of the Gruber Cosmology Prize for his part in the NASA Cosmic Background Explorer (COBE) measurements of the early universe.

2007 FUSE was decommissioned in October.

2007 Adam Riess, JHU Professor of Physics and Astronomy, is awarded the Gruber Cosmology Prize for providing evidence that the expansion of the universe is accelerating.

2008 July marked the official start of the Sloan Digital Sky Survey, part 3 (SDSS-III, 2008 – 2014).

2009 APL's Mini-RF instrument on the Chandrayaan-1 spacecraft maps the Moon's poles

2009 After years of delay and uncertainty, STS-125 (Atlantis) launched on Hubble Servicing Mission 4. Numerous JHU research staff were key players in preparations for the mission, real-time support activities, and post-installation calibration of the instruments, and John Grunsfeld performed three long space walks as part of on-orbit operations. In 2010, Grunsfeld became deputy director of STScI and was appointed a research professor in the department.

2010's

2010 APL's Compact Reconnaissance Imaging Spectrometer for Mars (CRISM) instrument points NASA's Mars Rovers to mineral deposits

2010 Charles L. Bennett, JHU Professor of Physics and Astronomy, is awarded the Shaw Prize in Astronomy for establishing the standard model of cosmology.

2011 APL’s MESSENGER spacecraft goes into orbit around Mercury

2011 Adam Riess, JHU Professor of Physics and Astronomy, is awarded the Nobel Prize for providing evidence that the expansion of the universe is accelerating.

2012 Charles L. Bennett, JHU Professor of Physics and Astronomy, is awarded the Gruber Cosmology Prize for the NASA WMAP space mission’s standard model of cosmology.

2013 Final results of the WMAP space mission are issued from the Homewood campus

2015 APL’s New Horizons missions takes first close up pictures of Pluto

About Us

Space@Hopkins unifies space-related activities across the institution including research in robotics, astronaut health, planetary sciences, solar physics, Earth science, spacecraft engineering, sensors, and astrophysics.
